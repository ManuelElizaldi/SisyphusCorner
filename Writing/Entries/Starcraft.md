Starcraft's path to becoming one of history's most impact video games is an interesting story about how economics, technology and culture intersect. 

This has its genesis in 1995 during a Korean economic boom funded by an exports based economy. This country was producing high tech products like semiconductors, electronics, ships, cars and telecommunications devices. Infrastructure was needed to remain competitive in this field at an international level. Additionally, the internet revolution was on the horizon and South Korea's Government wanted to come out victorious from it. 

To achieve this, thanks to the tax revenue from their exports, they started an expansive economic plan named KII (Korean Information Infrastructure) project that aimed at building optical networks around the country. This allowed Korea to gradually transition into a knowledge based economy. Many internet access points where funded and opened through out Korean cities. 

Then catastrophe struck in 1997 when the Asian Financial Crisis hit South Korea. The summary of this chapter in global economics *some transition here*. 

South East Asia was enjoying an economic boom that caused investors to trust in these countries and money flowed into these countries from foreigner investments. Currency pegging was placed, this economic tool matches the currency exchange rate to the dollar. Meaning, 1 US dollar was = x amount of won (Korean currency) - same for the other South East Asian countries. 

This allowed investors to enjoy the illusion of a risk free borrowing, a promise that you wouldn't lose money due to exchange rates when you withdrew your investment money. Then money started to flow aggressively and produced real expansion in some areas, but it also financed unproductive projects like government vanity infrastructure and real estate.

Then, stacked on top of this, the US started to raise interest rates domestically to cool off domestic inflation appreciating the US dollar and in consequence, appreciating the Asian pegged currencies. Since these economies were based in exports, their whole model started to crack since it became more expensive to export these goods. China's emerging economy also played a role in the crisis when they devalued their currency to attract investments and started their journey to become the world's factory.

One by one the domino pieces started to fall, Thailand was not able to pay for the burrowed money since the US dollar was more expensive and investors started to notice the vulnerabilities in the system. Foreign investors withdrew their money and their markets collapsed. This caused investors to withdraw their investments since they noticed that this pattern could be repeated in other countries. Trust was broken. A $100 billion dollar investment was not worth $170 billion dollars and there wasn't enough liquidity to pay all of this.  

South Korean conglomerates that enjoyed high liquidity now had massive amounts of debt they had to pay, lay off came, middle class was obliterated, but necessity is the mother of all innovations. The crisis was the gasoline that sparked flame to the internet revolution in Korea. Since the government had to help up the corporations, the government operated internet points of access were closed and small businesses started to open their own. These PC Bangs (internet cafes) rose from 100 in 1997 to over 13,000 by 1999. Infrastructure was already in place to build these internet cafes and this allowed the population of Korea to have internet access with powerful PCs. 

It is estimated that during this time, around 2000s, 57% of the population had internet access, compared to only 11% in the US. Then Starcraft I was released by Blizzard entertainment in 1998 and PC Bang owners wanted a game that could retain customers, so they usually kept up to date with new releases. The feedback loop starts. PC Bangs noticed the high demand for this strategy game, so they expanded.

On the historical & cultural aspects of the story, we have to revisit the 1910s. During 35 years, the Korean peninsula was a Japanese colony. In imperialist fashion Japan forced Koreans to learn Japanese at schools, use Japanese names and essentially *arrancar (look for an english word for this)* any assimilation to Korean identity. After they were liberated in 1945, but Korea was culturally wounded. To regain their national identity, nationalism was pushed and Japanese media was banned. Later down the road this gave way to an interesting economic situation that explains the popularity of PC games in Korea. 

There was also a ban on Japanese media placed in 1945, so the coast was clear for Starcraft to shock this market. 
During the 1990s consoles were the favored medium to play video games around the world, except in Korea. During this time consoles like the Nintendo 64 or the Playstation were not accessible and there was also a disdain for Japanese products. This removed any competition in the Korean market, so it was easier for PC Bangs to just plug in computers. Starcraft grew in a vacuum of video games.
