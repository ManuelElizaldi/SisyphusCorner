# Sister Mountains, Sister Hats
Another pair of gifted hats from my girlfriend, from the sister mountains of Snowbird and Alta in Utah in the Little Cottonwood Canyon. Both of them have a sportier look and have the resort's logos in the middle. The Snowbird hat's panels are made of fleece. This makes the hat a hybrid between a beanie and a cap, ideal for winter hikes. The Alta hat is my go to for running, weightlifting, bouldering or any other activity. The polyester makes it lightweight and breathable.

I've had the blessing of going skiing with friends and family, but I went to the sister mountains with my girlfriend. Our skiing trip starts at the grocery store were I feel like a kid at a candy shop buying calorie dense foods. Beans, bacon, eggs and biscuits. We eat these since we live in Texas and can't ski that often, so we make the most out of each day. We ensure our gas tank is full to skiing from opening hours until the last lift down.

In a selfless but selfish way I am the first one out of bed. I want to prepare everything as quickly as possible so that we can head to the mountains as early as we can. In the darkness of early morning I head to the kitchen to prepare our feast. Bacon goes in first, rendering its fat to make it crunchy. With a loud pop I open the Pillsbury biscuits  and into the 375°F oven they go. Scramble the eggs and heat up the beans. I start brewing the coffee, the smell of rendering fat and coffee mixing in the air firing up my body for the mountain day ahead. I refill our water bottles, pack some chips and make some sandwiches for our traditional mountain high picnic.   

Once all of that is done we pack the skis, boots and backpack into the truck and start heading west, taking the Little Cottonwood Rode. This leads to Alta and Snowbird. As I drive up the canyon I experience what it feels like to be hugged by the mountains. Nature to the left, right and in front of me. There's a small stream that flows next to the road that catches my attention. I am not sure what it is about bodies of water in these forest regions, but I can't resist their turquoise blue. I struggle to drive because I often get distracted by the huge pine trees, the birds flying high and the hope of spotting an elk somewhere in the snowy heights of the mountains.

Snowbird and Alta live right next to each other. I am not aware if there's other resorts like this in the world, but they are connected through one shared peak. There's a special lift ticket you can buy that allows skiing on both mountains. They call it the Altabird, it has a cool sound to it. These mountains are known to be factories of snow. The first time me and my girlfriend went there was mid April and there were sections of the mountain that still had knee high powder, prime for shredding.

I still remember the first time I went skiing back in 2017, my uncle mentioned that it was a good idea to book a ski class, so I did. My coach was an old mountaineer, probably around 75 years old. Agile like a snow fox with a goggle tan, skin aged by mountain high UV and a white thick mustache. The whole class went through the pizza fries, pizza fries drill. He showed us how to turn, which at the time made me realize my ankles were stiffer than ice. The coach gathered the class and gave us a PSA, insisting that all students should not go into blue runs. "Focus on greens and maybe next year, you can ride a blue." he remarked. 

After getting my quads torched in the learning zone with a 6% gradient slope, I yearned for a recovery beer. I met with my uncle after lunch, I mentioned what the coach said about avoiding blues and responded that his advice was useless. My uncle gathered his friends and immediately sat me in the lift heading up to the blue runs. I remember being surprised of having only a small little rail protecting me from the precipice. I naively thought to myself: How is this safe? I didn't want to voice out my concerns in front of my uncle and his friends. The fear I felt shook my knees and propelled me to bombard the crew with questions. Everyone ignored me, my uncle just calmly said "relax, you are going to have so much fun".

Years later I was exploring Alta with my girlfriend, our bodies felt stronger, easy to control after months of preparation for this ski season. Our quads felt like solid suspensions, skis responding well to the gentle carpet of snow. We moved from one part of the mountain to another hunting for adrenaline like snow leopards. I proposed going down Rock n Roll, a wavy blue run that felt like an adequate challenge for my girlfriend. I had already done this run before when my girlfriend was taking an easier route down, I knew she could do this.

You can do everything well, plan and execute to perfection. My girlfriend managed to go down this groovy run, skiing down several small snow hills, ramping up her speed and slowing with poise. But at the end of the run, she didn't see a small lump of snow and crashed, landing head first. I gave her time to recover, made sure nothing was broken and as I realized that she was crying, tenderly tried to comfort her saying everything was alright. After crying the frustration out, we skied down to the resting lodge.  

When that ordeal was done, we needed some well deserved recovery black coffee and our bologna sandwiches in sourdough. We ate our lunch with the mountains as our witness. We laid out the map in front of us, acting as a table and a tool to find our next prey. The caffeine was rushing through my blood stream, residual adrenaline from the previous runs demanded me to keep skiing. The level of detail in the map grounded me, it calmed my mind. The map of Alta had a multitude of lines of different colors. Each providing information about that section of the mountain. Some lines going left, other going right. Straight chutes, bowls, avalanche areas in transparent red. It was like looking at the roots of a tree forming knots.

Using this piece of art as our guide, we had a couple of candidates. Since I had already explored some parts of the steeper terrain, I declared Sugar Bowl as our next run. The controlled technique and self encouragement of my girlfriend were the only evidence I needed to know she was ready. I am not sure if she actually felt different or if it was the nerves doing the talking but she was against the idea at first. After some wrangling persuasion, reminding her of what she has done before, we made our way to Sugar Bowl. 

Sitting in this flying chair, anticipation increased the closer we got to the peak. Cold wind blew and brought a palpable tension with it. During the lift, I asked questions, said some cheap words of pseudo encouragement. I tried to fill the silence, perhaps now I was nervous for the baptism of snow that was about to occur. 

We got to the start of the run, my girlfriend seemed to have better composure. I hid my nervousness the best I could. I felt like a coach taking a struggling team to the final game of a punishing tournament. I told my girlfriend "Don't get fooled by the steepness, you got plenty of horizontal playground, just ski long parallel to the mountain and you should be good."

She gracefully skied, trusting her training. The tips of her skis responding to her command. Traces of her ability were imprinted on the snow. She was now gliding. The hesitation melted like snow in the warmer seasons to come. After a couple of turns she was at the bottom of the run, not believing what she just had done. I cheered her on and told her to turn around to witness the mountain she just conquered. 

The exploration of the mountain continued. To get to Mt. Baldy from Sugarloaf Peak we had to traverse a mile long catwalk named Devil's Way. During this flat transition run I told my girlfriend how proud I was about her black diamond run. She asked for some feedback which I provided to the best of my abilities. Nothing could have said, would've prepared her for what was to come. 

In front of us was a blue run, called Mambo, maybe foreshadowing the dance of emotions we were about to feel. This route had a steepness not even some of the blacks in Alta could boast. As I stood there, I wondered who the hell graded this run, this was for sure a black. Then I turned to see my girlfriend. A paralyzing fear took hold of her, she started repeating over and over that she could not do this. panic like a contagious disease took hold of me. 

There was no other way down, removing her skis and crawling down to a safer less steep area was not an option. It was time to get serious, I knew my girlfriend needed me. I had to be the pillar of comfort, but standing there with my skis planted in the snow I felt powerless. No suggestion or strategy I offered could help her. We had just skied down a black run, now we were stuck in the middle of a blue. I tried reminding her of her previous runs, the past is gone, fear is present. 

As she lied down in the snow, we see a little kid, probably 6 years old sending it. Stacking humiliation on top of the paralyzing panic. My girlfriend struggled to understand how it was so easy for that rascal. Then, from the snow emerged an old mountaineer ski patrol. I noticed the familiar beat up red, black and white jacket, a faded white mustache and the comforting goggle tan only years of high altitude mountains can bestow upon someone with immense experience. 

He's speech was tender, like a fire during a cold night. Every word spoken was precisely aimed at reassuring my girlfriend she was safe. I wanted his ability to comfort, but I knew only time could provide such powers. Captivated by the patrol's words, the powerlessness faded into the cold wind. 

Recovered from that scare, my girlfriend stood up. Standing near her I could hear how she was giving herself a pep talk, cheering herself. She slowly glided through the steepness, long horizontal gliding. I remained on top of the mountain, having a front row seat. 

I caught up with her before the end of Mambo, confidence and pride for my girlfriend taking a hold of me once more. She gave a cry of joy because she was done with this run. I hugged her and we continued exploring the mountain.
