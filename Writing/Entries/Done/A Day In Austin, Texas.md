### "I went out without a plan and the city _gave_ me a day"

It was not a hard week, it was just a work week. Finished a project that didn't end up being finished, as it usually goes with projects. Revisions based on new requests. Code changes to accommodate for new information, dependency updates or bug fixes caused by a slip in my QA. 

Time flies in front of me. I clearly remember how when I first moved to this apartment, there was a barren plot of land which I could see from my desk into my window. After some months, movement started. Day by day, new material, scaffolding, heavy machinery, new pieces adding to this puzzle. I would day dream looking at the workers, performing their tasks like little ants. 

Each passing week gave way to progress in the construction. Hinting at what was being built. I enjoyed trying to guess what they were constructing, perhaps a library or a shopping center? The skeleton was there, but the muscles which provide functionality were absent, I had no real way of knowing from a distance. 

A year and a half later, the building was completed. Every week day at 11:00 AM I now enjoy watching the kids run into the patio where the construction workers mounted a playground for students to enjoy their recess.

Time has been telling me how working from home is affecting me. I feel like a cattle dog, filled with energy ready to wrangle the world to my pleasing, but for most of my days, my world is inside my apartment. It sometimes feels like I am an extra in a movie, where I am inside of the film, but not being part of the main event. 

This apartment acts like a comforting dog crate that provides me with security and a respectable good earning job. A social claustrophobia caused by the comforts of having my office 10 steps away from my bed. 

I need to make an active effort to get out of the house and let that sun provide its vitamin D. Comfort is persuasive in this matter. I tango with it, avoiding submission.

Since my girlfriend had an event in her hometown, I had to bid her farewell and was left with my own volition. I grab my cellphone and open Google Maps. Hoping this rectangle of infinite information can provide an antidote to the anxiety of free time. 

After years of trying my best to embody Anthony Bourdain's philosophy of living, I have amassed many 'Want to go' places on my Google Map. Little green flags marking my culinary curiosities. I grew up in a small town, where you involuntarily commit to not making a choice. A couple yesterdays ago it was easy to decide what to do with time, because there was few options. 

Now I live in a living and evolving paradox of choice. I have this Friday to myself and I don't know what to do with it. Sunset is in two hours, so I decided to hedge my luck and go for a safe bet. A classic Austin activity, Barton Springs. 

I arrived just in time, unpacked my towel, lay down in the grass, read a little bit of Hunter S. Thompson's Hell's Angels and observed the people around me. Jacked dudes, small bikinis and some topless hippies, all classic Austin sightings. 

I jumped in the cold water, decided to swim a lap, starting with freestyle strokes but resorting to breast stroke when I got tired after 50 yards. I have never been a good swimmer, but being fair with myself I have never practiced it seriously. I do hope to run a triathlon someday. 

After my attempt at swimming, I remained in the cold water for some minutes. I held to the railing placed on the sides of the spring fed pool, floating like a lily pad. I remember I heard somewhere that spring water has mythical powers. The minerals are supposed to help your body, not sure with what exactly but I do enjoy the cold of the natural water. Even 11,000 years ago, the native people of these lands used to bathe in these waters. 

The sun concluded its march upon the sky and the moon took its place. I dried myself, put on my flip flops, jeans over my swimming trunks, rocked the no t-shirt look and walked back to my car. As I traversed the parking lot of Barton Springs, I stumbled upon an out doors ballet recital. Wanting to be part of the cultural scene of this city, I sat down in the grass again and admired the ability of dancers to spin and spin, high in the air with grace and control. By situations likes these, I understand how Austin won its nickname of "Live Music Capital of the World".

I felt like I had my fair share of classical music and ballet and now needed a beer. I have some strong candidate places, but taking advantage of being close to downtown, I made my way to The Cloak Room. A dive bar next to the Texas Capitol, supposedly both structures connected by underground tunnels. 

Any place that has a quirk or a urban folk tale attached to it, tickles my curiosity and makes it hundred times more attractive. When I arrived, Google Maps said the bar was inside an old looking but renovated building, decorated with the black and silver Texas Historical Marker in the façade. 

I had been curious about this bar, but now I was intrigued. I walked around the property, searching for the bar and then noticed a painted hand pointing down with the word "bar" on top of it. I realized the bar was on the basement level of this historical building. As I descended down the stairs, my wonder rose. 

There were few patrons inside, I assumed the usual crowd for a Friday night, no one sitting at the bar so I made myself comfortable there. Whenever I go to bars I usually carry a small notebook, pocket sized. As I have mentioned in previous essays, workouts are a great source of inspiration, fertile ground for ideas, but dive bars are a collection of characters. College Students with cheap hard hitting drinks, tired and divorced business men, punks, misfits and wanna be writers. I need to be locked and loaded.

I ordered a Texas two step, a Buffalo Trace Whiskey shot with a beer as a chaser. The bartender asked me how my day was, which I proceeded to describe. I mentioned how I saw a random ballet outside Barton Springs and showed her a picture. Surprised she said "No way! That's my friend right there." and pointed at one of the dancers in my picture. I thought to myself "What is the probability of this happening?".

We continued chatting, she mentioned that she was born and raised in Austin, a unicorn. Then told me how she studied archaeology in London, then moved to Germany for an internship. Taking a leap of faith into a profession which the status quo would consider too risky. Listening to her stories about Berlin from my side of the bar, I couldn't help but compare my life to hers. In contrast mine followed a clear line answering to the "this is the next logical step for you".

My notebook was closed, laying on the bar, next to my beer. I was immersed in the bartender's stories, weapon out but shell shocked.  The material I was looking for gracefully walked in front of my sights. I probed her for more details, picking up any little crumb I could use for a story or an essay.

The conversation flowed back to me, she asked about how I found this place. I was not able to say precisely since I couldn't remember, but I asked her about the mythical hidden tunnels connecting the bar to the Texas Capitol. She replied "I can't confirm nor deny". Was this something bartenders in the Cloak Room trained to say? I might be breaking an omertà of Saloon Society, but she told me how Congress men would meet their lovers at this bar during lunch break. She wasn't supposed to tell me any of this. 

Thirst for a beer was replaced with hunger for food. I decided to leave my car parked in front of The Cloak Room and walk around Congress Avenue. Letting my sense of smell guide me, although my hearing took hold. A lap steel guitar howled and called my name. I walked toward the music and spotted a neon light acting as a lighthouse with the word "Saturn". My target was acquired. 

Walking into the dim lighting I saw funky decor, some fake palm trees and a caricature of Saturn painted on the wall. I sat down at this "Cocktail Social Room", a stylish place without acting pretentious. Ordered a Japanese Hot Dog, while the troubadours played. I enjoyed my dinner, it was an umami bomb detonating with a delicious savory sausage, furikake and mustard. A balanced combination of cultures. Fitting for Austin. I quickly texted my girlfriend, saying I will bring her to try this some day.

I scribbled down in my notebook the collection of events I just lived. Each a blessing I received, each holding potential for a story. The cold healing waters at Barton Springs, conversations about lives well lived, the longing to share good food with loved ones. Every bullet point an attempt at not letting ideas fade away. While I wrote, I heard the rock country band playing a song about the Texas floods of last year.
