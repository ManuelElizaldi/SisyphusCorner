The day is Wednesday, June 10th, 2026. I wake up having barely slept from excited anxiousness. It was a sort of a happy anxiety. A feeling of knowing something will happen and I wanted it to happen. I started my day by logging into work. Running my reports and then patiently waited for 11:00 am. 

My cloud engineer/solutions architect certification exam was scheduled at 12:00 pm. 8 months, 243 days, around 500 hours of diligent studying. Each rep was a *no* to any tempting distractions. Day after day of studying an hour to two hours a day. Everything aimed at a promise for better work. I am not sure what metric I am using for 'better', but definitely something more fulfilling, more challenging. 

Always evolving, for it is in my nature to look for the next step. Rest does not come easy to me. I feel like I am always on the go, not sure if this behavior comes from the grasp of optimization gurus on social media or if it has always been there. A nagging sensation of the "what's next" lives rent free inside of me. It also could be excitement for living. As I write this and glance at my library, searching for words or perhaps inspiration, I realize I have the evidence of this right smack in front of me. 

Every time I look at book reviews on YouTube or Instagram, my 'want to read list' grows and grows like roots taking hold of my brain exerting pressure. I check out my list, and think to myself, "so many books and so little life." There is so much I want to read, so much I want to do. Perhaps that thirst for experience is the cause of my unrest. 

Regardless, I am sitting at my desk waiting for the time I can start heading out to Austin Community College testing center. Due to excitement, responsibility or nerves I arrive an hour early. I take this time to relax. I once read in a book called "Learning How to Learn" by Barbara Oakley, that taking a pause from studying allows the brain to trigger something called: *diffuse brain*. This is when the mind works in the background to plant all the seeds of knowledge. Connections between neural pathways are established. Basically, this is when all the hard work pays off and your brain locks in the new studied material. 

I walk through campus, surprised at how many different characters I can spot. Some carry an untroubled swagger. There were others who I could plainly see that were school staff. Some other dudes were just sitting down, focused on their assignments in their laptops. 

Each with its own worries, demands to the universe, reasons to live and perhaps just wandering about. The wave of sonder that I was experiencing placed me in a tranquil space, forgetting about my exam. It put me in a state of relaxation. A calm before the storm type of deal. 

As I continued my pondering walk, I stumble upon many pictures taken by students hung throughout the building. The pictures on the walls resemble the variety of people I could see. Each its own genre of photography. There were some modern matcha drinking cowboys, pictures of old parlors embodying the spirit of "old Austin, Texas", what looked like a dwarf stripper and some random modern art photography. An absurdist challenge, demanding you to give it your own meaning. 

At 11:45 am I make way to the testing center. A new wave of emotions irradiate my body now. I feel like an high-performance athlete. The spotlight is on me, awaiting my cunning moves. My strategy backed up by the countless repetitions I executed these last 8 months. I was given some simple instructions by the testing staff and shown to the computer where I was to take my exam. 

Time started ticking, 2 hours, 65 questions to test my 8 months of work. I wonder, how so much effort can be distilled into 2 hours. Like a football player taking a penalty. Years of training that are justified by the process of scoring a winning match goal. 

I worked through each question, some being obvious only through my arduous study. Others, presented a challenge. This friction between the known questions and the hard ones summoned an internal familiar little voice, that said "You should've studied harder." A grueling voice that has been with me, through highs and lows. A dear companion that has tormented me and crowned me with fruitful rewards. Showing up to demand more of me, pushing me to my higher potential. But also, a punisher who flagellates when I don't reach my objective. 

Since high school I have been managing this tug of war relationship with my internal judge. Its genesis occurred during a rebellious phase. I was getting close to being expelled from my high school. My family and teachers gave me an ultimatum, correct my act or face the guillotine and get kicked out. I didn't want to leave that school, all of my friends were there and I thought that could be the most humiliating thing to happen to me. 

So I had to pull my big boy pants up and shape up. Slowly, my behavior started to change, I started saying 'good morning' to teachers, I focused on my assignments and I began to lay the bricks for a transformation, each brick reaffirming the presence of the judge. Throughout this process, my internal voice guided me through my decision making, each aimed at improving my reputation with professors.

Fast forward a couple years of finessing and diligent studying, I graduated with honors from my high school, earning a scholarship for college education. The internal judge was beside me, saving me from inevitable doom, but this voice got too comfortable in the deep nooks of my brain, becoming a consciousness programming that never left.

After 15 years, the judge resurfaces now and then. Some days to remind me of my goals, like a horse with blinders, the judge places my attention where it should be. Other times, the voice appears and lashes out its punishing complaints about not studying hard enough. There are also times where I can feel this voice when I am resting, reminding me that time is finite, my most valuable resource. It demands of me to take advantage of the privilege of time, to forget about rest. 

I harnessed my energy and kept my attention on the questions, making my best efforts to answer as best I could. After the 2 hours passed, I was done with my exam. I could immediately feel a weight being lifted from my shoulders, I was free, for a bit. Now it was time to wait for my test results. Due to the challenging nature of the exam I was not expecting much, the only thing I felt was gratitude from not having to study for a couple of days. My mind and my body needed a break. 

I grabbed my stuff, headed towards my car and drove to the HEB bar. An alcohol oasis next to a grocery store where you can find cheap beers, a rarity in Austin. I have always wanted to slam down a couple of beers and then do my weekly grocery shopping but the opportunity has never presented itself.

I ordered a Modelo and sat down to decompress from the mental strain.  I sipped my Mexican lager while I sent some voice messages to my girlfriend, friends and family. I told them the truth, that that exam was hard and I really didn't expect much. Every single one of them believed more in me than I did in myself. 

I sipped my beer while I overheard the conversations of other patrons. Some spoke of the World Cup being around the corner. Some gentleman was conducting some sort of business transaction on the phone. I looked up and watched the baseball game that was playing on the TV, the Yankees were bullying the Cleveland Guardians, 8 - 2. 

After another sip of my indulgence, I noticed, no cheering, no applause, no flagellation. The test was done, unknown result, but for now the judge was not present.
