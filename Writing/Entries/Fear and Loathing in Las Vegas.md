1. Duke and Gonzo behave outrageously the entire book — but who are the real predators in the story, them or Las Vegas itself?

Las Vegas didn't make them predators, but it was fertile ground for outrageous behavior. They went there with a working assignment that was used as an excuse to stock up on drugs and intentions to get as twisted as possible.

Where other cities punish outrageous behavior, Vegas celebrates it, as long as you are a big tipper. No where else could you get a suite with sound proof walls that allows you to go through a bad acid trip screaming the White Rabbit song and demanding your friend to kill you in the bath tub.

Duke wrangled a sickening paranoia, but still got away with not paying the $25 an hour for 5 days bills for room service. It was easy for him to fuel up to continue his drug binge. Reckless driving through the strip is treated as bumper cars at the carnival. 

All of this sponsored by their work assignment to which Duke, thanks to the drugs and alcohol had little content to cover. Just like the Broken Window Theory, Vegas encourages wild behavior. Vegas is a show for the twisted and depraved, come here with ill intentions and you'll be a star.

2. Thompson is brutal about the failure of the 60s counterculture. Does he seem nostalgic, bitter, or both?

There is a failure in the hippie counter culture. There was a promise of peace and freedom. You could be or do anything you wanted and all of your efforts amounted to something good. When they reached the mountain of self idealized virtue they realized there was no throne waiting for their benevolence. No checkered flag marking the finish line. When there's no enemy to fight, no cause to aim the collective efforts, direction is lost. Emptiness replaces the dream. Peace and love replaced by the surveillance.

3. The book never tells you what the American Dream _is_ — only where it isn't. After nine chapters, what do you think Thompson believed it was supposed to be?

A dead ideal. The entire book they spent searching. They were trying to document the mint 400 race, but Raoul only captured the beginning. Then at the Drug convention, he wanted to go into the lion's cave, only to realize how ill prepared the cops were to face the drug epidemic. So in reality, the American Dream is a constant search for something that doesn't exist. The american dream, according to Duke, might be the constant struggle to fill a void within ourselves with external input. Luxury, drugs, travel, etc. All of these wont grant you any dreams. 

4. Duke is a journalist who produces almost no journalism. What does Thompson seem to be saying about the press?

The press is also a failed institution, we have a clear example of this when he is narrating the story about a dead soldier in Vietnam. He probably died in the hands of the Viet cong but the press will never release that. The truth is sold for the highest bidder in order to craft a story.
