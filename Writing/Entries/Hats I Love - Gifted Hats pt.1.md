
# Boston Bruins
This was a gift from my girlfriend, a Boston's Bruins hat that she bought when she went to Boston to present her research study on risk fall prevention. This is your classic baseball hat design with the team's logo on the center. Since it is black and minimalist I can pair it with most outfits. 

I live in a city where it is not uncommon to meet people from all around and I usually get asked if I am from Boston. It happens more that I thought it would, but I find it funny how you can find your tribe, your people by the pieces of clothing you are wearing. 

Even though I am a Dallas All Stars fan, I have used this hat proudly since it is a cherished gift. From using it I have discovered another personal curiosity. I wear things that were gifted to me more often than the stuff I buy and I also tend to take better care of them. 

Hockey is a sport that has a special place in my heart. I look back at it with some nostalgia since I used to go to watch my home town's local team: The Laredo Bucks, with my father and some friends. Reminiscing about this time, I find interesting how two complete opposite coexist in surreal fashion, a hockey team calling an arid south Texas town its home. 

The team's logo had a cool looking white tail dear in gold and blue with the word "Laredo", where the L was shaped like a hockey stick. The combination of colors and shapes made a cool contrast with the team's jersey. 'Till this day, I am still hunting for a Laredo Bucks Jersey.

I am not entirely sure why I started liking this sport, I can't remember if it was my dad's idea to take me to a game or if it was mine. Maybe I started liking it because I used to watch Rocket Power on Nickelodeon and there were some episodes where they played street hockey. Regardless, I remained a fan after all these years because of the frigid raw speed and violence of this sport. 

I recall being 10 years old and entering the hockey arena for the first time. I immediately felt the temperature drop and the noise of the crowd was almost deafening. At that moment I didn't understand why these people were cheering with such intense passion. 

This frigid arena felt more like a colosseum where you could feel the tension growing in the air. Athletes moving at inhuman speeds on the ice chasing the puck. Defenders slamming the enemy into the walls. Each play approaching the inevitable storm of violence. "We want blood, we want blood!" demanded the crowd. I looked around me, but everybody, even my father was intoxicated with the display of power of these athletes. Then the storm arrived when a fight broke between the players. Clinch, left hook, clinch, upper cut and the blood arrived. I jumped up and screamed in excitement.  


# Sister Mountains, Sister Hats

# It is easy to love the mountains, it takes soul to love the Texas Brush Country - El Venadito Ranch

Every trip to the ranch starts before sunrise, where in the darkness of the early morning, excitement alone is enough to wake you up. Still, there's an obligatory stop at a connivence store to buy some cheap coffee. 

Adventure lies around 30 to 40 minutes away from town. This gives me and my friends enough distance to disconnect ourselves from civilization. Any worries that we might be carrying along, are set on pause. 

To get to The Venadito Ranch you have to cross a trail that has been marked by usage. Making our way through the path, sipping on our gas station coffee we remember past trips, reminiscing of a time that used to be. In the background we have Lalo Mora, Charley Crockett and other country and ranchera artists serenading us. 

We slowly make our way through this veranda of mesquite and nopales, seeing jack rabbits blaze of into the brush and road runners speeding in front of us. With the sun rising in the horizon, we have entered into the domain of the White Tail deer. 

Once we arrive, we put our affairs in order. Unload the groceries, which are made up of mostly precious beer and the carne asada we plan on cooking later that day. We offer our help to the ranch hand carrying out any of his tasks, then after all of that is said and done, we prep everything for the next day's hunt. 

Winter in the northeastern part of Mexico means hunting season is a go. With shorter days, deer get *perked up/jacked up* (not sure what to put here yet) and start moving from one place to another looking for does to court. improvement here -> This time of year also before white tail deer shed their horns. 

have always seen hunting as one of humanities oldest rituals. In a world where industrialization has optimized food pipelines it is easy to forget that we were once part of the natural ecosystem, living for and by it. Early civilizations had religions and rituals revolve around hunting and it was a part where community was built. Where children became men. The comforts of a economically developed society has amputated our connection to nature and this ritual. 



Hunting day starts early, earlier than the trip to the ranch the day before. At 4:00 am you already can smell someone prepping coffee from the warmth of the bed. Usually we all sleep in the same room just like we used to do back in the day when we were kids and did pajama parties. Someone would crack a joke about how early it is and then eventually make our way to the kitchen attracted by the smell of the coffee.

People start prepping their gear, jackets, rifles, binoculars, cameras and intrepid ones prep their hunting bows. Pack everything into the trucks. After all these years, people are still using bows to acquire their food. We review yesterday's agreed plan, dividing up in groups. Small groups since we don't want to scare off the pray and head out to the blinds. These are small, hardly comfy 5’ x 5’ special rooms built to hide from the deer. Nobody mentions this, but hunting is 90% waiting. A sort of meditative wait where I pay attention to the land infront of me. 

Hunting changes from region to region, but in Northeastern Mexico we hunt Whitetail deer, these animals have super powers, great hearing and amazing sight. Any small movement or impertinent joke made during the wait is heard by them and they run away as fasts as they can. So prudence is of the most importance here. 

While waiting, my mind acts like a magnet of ideas. For example, I remember the last time I was at the venadito ranch me and my friend were at the blind and I was holding the rifle I case we saw something. I started thinking how this small tool holds so much power. Power to destroy and power to create. A tool that must be handles with the upmost respect. It can provide food for your family, but it can also be used for more nefarious means. 

That time around I didn't get lucky but my bow wielding friend did. This was his first time hunting with a bow after months of preparation. He was using an 80 pound bow that required should, back and grip strength and he managed to (put down? can't recall the correct term for killing) a white tail deer that provided enough meat to make tamales for Christmas. We helped him pack up the body of the deer, with much respect for his sacrfice and then handed it to the vaquero of the ranch. 

I had the opportunity to learn how to skin an animal, although I grew up around this culture, I never had the curiousity to learn how to properly clean an animal. Watching this slow ritual being performed with the most respect and care increased my devotion to this long practiced human tradition.
