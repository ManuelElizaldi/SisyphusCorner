
# Boston Bruins
This was a gift from my girlfriend, a Boston's Bruins hat that she bought when she went to Boston to showcase her research study on risk fall prevention. This is your classic baseball hat design with the team's logo on the center. This is an easy to wear hat since I can pair with most outfits. 

I live in a city where it is not uncommon to meet people from all around and most of the times when I wear this hat I get asked if I am from Boston. It happens more that I thought it would, but I find it funny how you can find your tribe, your people by the pieces of clothing you are wearing. 

Although I am a Dallas All Stars fan I wear this hat proudly since it is a cherished gift. I have come to noticed that I wear things that were gifted to me more often than the stuff I buy, I also take more care of them. 

Hockey is a sport that has a special place in my heart. I look back at it with some nostalgia since I used to go to watch my home town's local team: The Laredo Bucks with my father and some friends. Reminiscing about this time, it seems surreal that an arid south Texas town had a hockey team. 

The team's logo had a really cool looking white tail dear in gold and blue with the word "Laredo", where the L was shaped like a hockey stick. The combination of colors and shapes made a cool contrast with the team's jersey.  

# Sister Mountains, Sister Hats

# It is easy to love the mountains, it takes soul to love the Texas Brush Country - El Venadito Ranch

Every trip to the ranch starts before sunrise, where in the darkness of the early morning, excitement alone is enough to wake you up. Still, there's an obligatory stop at a connivence store to buy some cheap coffee. 

Adventure lies around 30 to 40 minutes away from town. This gives me and my friends enough distance to disconnect ourselves from civilization. Any worries that we might be carrying along, are set on pause. 

To get to The Venadito Ranch you have to cross a trail that has been marked by the usage *(maybe another word here?)*. Making our way through the path, sipping on our gas station coffee we remember past trips, reminiscing of a time that used to be. In the background we have Lalo Mora, Charley Crockett and other country and ranchera artists serenading our trek. 

We slowly make our way through this veranda of mesquite and nopales, seeing jack rabbits blaze of into the brush and road runners speeding in front of us. With the sun rising in the horizon, we have entered into the domain of the White Tail deer. 

Once we arrive, we put our affairs in order. Unload the groceries, which is made up of mostly our precious beer and the carne asada we plan on cooking later that day. We offer our help to the ranch hand carrying out any of his tasks, then after all of that is said and done, we prep everything for the next day's hunt. 

Winter in the northeastern part of Mexico means hunting season is a go. With shorter days, deer get *perked up/jacked up* and start moving from one place to another looking for does to court. This time of year is also before white tail deer shed their horns. 


I have always seen hunting as one of humanities oldest rituals. In a world where industrialization has optimized food pipelines it is easy to forget that we were once part of the natural ecosystem, living for and by it. Early civilizations had religions and practices revolve around hunting and it was a part where community was built. Where children became men. *maybe give an example here?*. The comforts of a economically developed society has amputated our connection to nature and this ritual.
