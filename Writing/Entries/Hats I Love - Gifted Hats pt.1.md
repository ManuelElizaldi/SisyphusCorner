
# Boston Bruins
This was a gift from my girlfriend, a Boston's Bruins hat that she bought when she went to Boston to present her research study on risk fall prevention. This is your classic baseball hat design with the team's logo on the center. Since it is black and minimalist I can pair it with most outfits. 

I live in a city where it is not uncommon to meet people from all around and I usually get asked if I am from Boston. It happens more that I thought it would, but I find it funny how you can find your tribe, your people by the pieces of clothing you are wearing. 

Even though I am a Dallas All Stars fan, I have used this hat proudly since it is a cherished gift. From using it I have discovered a personal curiosity. I wear things that were gifted to me more often than the stuff I buy and I also tend to take better care of them. 

Hockey is a sport that has a special place in my heart. I look back at it with some nostalgia since I used to go to watch my home town's local team: The Laredo Bucks, with my father and some friends. Reminiscing about this time, I find interesting how two complete opposite coexist in surreal fashion, a hockey team calling an arid south Texas town its home. 

The team's logo had a cool looking white tail dear in gold and blue with the word "Laredo", where the L was shaped like a hockey stick. The combination of colors and shapes made a cool contrast with the team's jersey. 'Till this day, I am still hunting for a Laredo Bucks Jersey.

I am not entirely sure why I started liking this sport, I can't remember if it was my dad's idea to take me to a game or if it was mine. Maybe I started liking it because I used to watch Rocket Power on Nickelodeon and there were some episodes where they played street hockey. Regardless, I remained a fan after all these years because of the frigid raw speed and violence. 

I recall being 10 years old and entering the hockey arena for the first time. I immediately felt the temperature drop and the noise of the crowd was almost deafening. At that moment I didn't understand why these people were cheering with such intense passion. 

This frigid arena felt more like a Colosseum where you could feel the tension growing in the air. Athletes moving at inhuman speeds on the ice chasing the puck. Defenders slamming the enemy into the walls. Each play approaching the inevitable storm of violence. "We want blood, we want blood!" demanded the crowd. I looked around me, but everybody, even my father was intoxicated with the display of power of these athletes. Then the storm arrived when a fight broke between the players. Clinch, left hook, clinch, upper cut and the blood splattered. I jumped up and screamed in excitement.

By now, the Laredo Bucks are no more. Last time I checked the team was sold off to some other long gone team in a completely different state. From time to time I hop on eBay feeling optimistic, hoping that some niche Texas hockey collector is selling that jersey. A part of me looking for an anchor to that frigid Colosseum, and the other knows  that the feeling might live in a hockey arena in Boston.

# It is easy to love the mountains, it takes soul to love the Texas Brush Country - El Venadito Ranch

Every trip to the ranch starts before sunrise, where in the darkness of the early morning, excitement alone is enough to wake you up. Still, there's an obligatory stop at a convenience store to buy some cheap coffee. 

Adventure lies around 30 to 40 minutes away from town. This gives me and my friends enough distance to disconnect ourselves from civilization. Any worries that we might be carrying along, are set on pause. 

To get to The Venadito Ranch you have to cross a trail that has been marked by time. Making our way through the path, sipping on our gas station coffee we remember past trips, reminiscing of a time that used to be. In the background we have Lalo Mora, Charley Crockett and other country and ranchera artists serenading us. 

We slowly make our way through this veranda of mesquite and nopales, seeing jack rabbits blaze of into the brush and road runners speeding in front of us. With the sun rising in the horizon, we have entered into the domain of the White Tail deer. 

Once we arrive, my friend's dad gave me this hat. A gift for the 2022 season. It has the skull of a white tail deer in black, the Mexican flag and my name on it. I have used it so much that the color has started to fade.

We put our affairs in order. Unload the groceries, we offer our help to the ranch hand carrying out any of his tasks. Then we prep everything for the next day's hunt. We drive around and walk to survey the area where the hunting is to take place. We clear the area and sketch out a plan. 

After all of that is said and done, time has passed and we are offered a burned sky stretching in front of us. From here, in the middle of the brush, the sky seems infinite.

Going to the ranch will always mean that there's going to be cooking with fire. First we have to gather the wood, usually mesquite since it is in abundance in the brush country. Then we unpack the groceries we brought. Different cuts of meats, arrachera, sausage, northern needle (as it is known in Laredo), beans, tortillas and vegetables to make salsa.

Like highly coordinated ants with the single objective of eating and drinking, tasks are split up - almost without talking. Anybody helps where there's help needed. Someone preps the wood and fire. Some other person seasons the meat and then somebody chars the vegetables to make salsa 'molcajeteada', everybody silently hoping it is as spicy as the people from this region of Mexico. Finally, the friend that didn't end up with a task serves jokes, beers and makes drinks. 

The mesquite smoke rises into the starry night. There's little to no noise out there, the occasional animal bustling through the brush. Then we get romantic with a sparkle of nostalgia and the troubadours come out of the Bluetooth speakers and continue their serenade. 

Hunting day starts early, earlier than the trip to the ranch the day before. At 4:00 am I can already smell someone prepping coffee from the warmth of my bed. Usually we all sleep in the same room with 6 or so beds, just like we used to do back in the day when we were kids and did pajama parties. Eventually I make it out of bed and into the kitchen where we I drink some dark coffee.  

People start prepping their gear, jackets, rifles, binoculars, cameras and intrepid ones prep their hunting bows. After all these years, people are still using bows to acquire their food. We then pack everything into the trucks.  We review yesterday's agreed plan, divide up into small groups since we don't want to scare off the pray and head out to the blinds. These are small, hardly comfy around 6'x 6' special rooms built to hide from the deer. Nobody mentions this, but hunting is 90% waiting. A sort of meditative wait where I pay attention to the land in front of me. 

In Northeastern Mexico we hunt Whitetail deer, these animals have super powers, great hearing and precise sight. Any small movement or impertinent joke made during the wait is heard by them and they run away as fasts as they can. So prudence is of the most importance here. 

While waiting, my mind acts like a magnet of ideas. I remember the last time I was at the Venadito Ranch, surrounded by dark silence of the brush country, I held the rifle at the ready. I could feel its cold metal barrel and wooden handle demanding respect. A tool of such power must be used with care. It can provide food and protection, but it can be aimed at nefarious means as well.

This time around I didn't get lucky but my bow wielding friend did. This was his first time hunting with a bow after months of preparation. He was using an 80 pound bow that required shoulder, back and grip strength. He managed to hunt a white tail deer that provided enough meat to make tamales for Christmas. We helped him pack up the body of the deer in the truck, with much respect for the animal's sacrifice and then handed it to the vaquero of the ranch. 

I had the opportunity to learn how to skin an animal, although I grew up around this culture, I was never shown how to properly process an animal. Watching this slow ritual being performed with the most respect and care increased my devotion to this long practiced human tradition. 

After unloading the deer, the vaquero made two small insertions on the back legs of the animal. This was done to hook the deer up a special hanger that made it easier for him to handle the animal. The vaquero displayed a level of care and attention that is only gained after thousands of repetitions. 

Each stroke carefully made, making it look as if it was second nature for him. In awe at how skillful he was preparing for this, I asked him if he could teach me how to do it. As it is normal for people with great understanding and huge amounts of experience, he was more than happy to show me how this was done.

He started his teaching explaining how important it is to sharpen your knife. He showed me the boning knife he was using, which the handle already showed the years of use. "A dull knife will only make your life harder" he had said. Maybe the whole impression of this experience made me look deeper at this phrase, but something inside me told me that maybe he was not just talking about a knife. 

Since this was my first time I was only observing, he made a 'Y' cut going down from the back legs, then stopping at its testicles. There he cut around them and kept cutting down to its neck. The sharpness of the knife made the deer's fur seem like paper. The blade was effortlessly gliding through the skin. He then cut around the neck of the animal, preserving it for a trophy.

He removed the insides, removed the skin from the muscle, extracted the head carefully for later processing and then, the job was done, we had meat for our tamales. His skill allowed him to only dirty his hands and his knife.
