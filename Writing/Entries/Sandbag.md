
The clock marks 18:00. I am done with work, brain a bit fried from building data pipelines, the vibrations of stress receding throughout my body. I might be mentally tired, but the muscles are ready to hustle.

I have a portable gym sitting at the back of my car, some friends have pointed out to me how messy my trunk looks, but the two 36.11 pound chimp face shaped kettle bells, jumping rope and 100 pound sandbag provide a consistency. No workout left unskipped when I can easily workout from my car. This is enough equipment for me to get in a solid workout, plus this is not too much stuff so setting it up at parks is fairly easy. I can also travel to visit my family out of town without missing a workout. 

Today I am taking my mobile gym to the top of my apartment's parking lot to train with Austin's buildings stretching in the horizon with a burnt sky as a stage.

I park at the far end, open the trunk and unload the sandbag and kettle bells. I grab the jumping rope and warm up with simple and double jumps for 5 to 10 minutes. The impact of my feet hitting the concrete primes my legs for the toll that's to come. 

Sand, resting motionless, not wanting to be picked up, 100 pounds of entrapped in this bag. I stare at this mass, hesitate to start my workout.

Friction lies at the begging. Be it a blank page before an essay, approaching a girl you want to talk to or working out. Once you are in the fray, momentum carries you forward. 

As the setting sun warms my body while I jump rope, I remember that I read somewhere that people in Scotland have a map of historic lifting stones. These rocks that have been resting in place for millennia, wait for a brave soul to interrupt their slumber, accepting their challenge. Rocks that turn children into men. This is enough to get me fired up.

I start my workout with farmer carries, grabbing the sandbag with my hands, digging my fingers into its fabric to get a solid hold. Lift, then get into a squatting position I then place the sandbag in my lap, hug it as hard as I can and stand up. I feel the sandbag in my torso, my ribs crushing my lungs, adding a breathing challenge to this exercise. I make an effort not to think, just move. My legs slowly move forward, it takes me back to rugby games when I had to carry the ball and the opposition's defense hung to my body, making an effort to bring me down, demanding the ball back. Pure conviction of strength keeping the momentum going. 

100 ft of resisting the pain, 50 ft in one direction and 50 ft back to where I started.
