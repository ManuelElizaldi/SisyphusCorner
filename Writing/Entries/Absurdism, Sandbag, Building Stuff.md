As I feel the warmth of the afternoon in my body, waiting for my heart rate to settle, I stare at my hands. A burning sensation from holding the hard fabric of the sandbag that is kicking my ass during this workout. In the wait for the next round, I noticed the familiar ink stains that have been a common occurrence in my body ever since I started using fountain pens. 

When I grab the sandbag, the stains are oddly comforting, a paradoxical sight to behold. These hands wrote an essay a few moments ago and now are carrying a hundred pound sandbag. I start my second round, lift the sandbag, hinge at the hip, place it in my lap while in a squatting position, then hug it tight to my body. The diaphragm gets crushed by the weight and the pressure I exert by holding it. I violently explode, push my hips forward and throw the bag overhead.

It doesn't seem to matter how long I observe and analyze the inner workings on my mind, there will always be something new to discover. A tornado of ideas that sometimes is as serene as a pond in the high mountains. Then becomes an earthquake of activity that is hard to control. Spontaneous thundery rain that may bloom into projects, essays or just fade into nonexistence.

I have come to notice that workouts are the perfect weather station to monitor new ideas. During this session I remembered how I had to put this bag together. I ordered it online and just got the empty bag - a part of me expected the thing to be already filled up, but alas. I had to go to Home Depot and buy the filling, before this I had no idea there were different types of sand, leveling sand, play sand, paver sand. The infinite universe of options just lead me to google what was best. 

Bought 2 sacks that equal to a hundred pounds of dry play sand. Another perplexing thought, dry sand. I thought all sand was dry, or at least the one sold here. Filling the sandbag for my workouts was simple, I only had to remove the protective Velcro, open a zipper for an inner bag and fill it up. In these simple steps I had created my own self destruction device. 

The sandbag hits the concrete making a loud 'thump' that shakes the floor of the parking lot where I am training. The noise zaps me back to the moment. Some dust or perhaps sand flows upward from the impact. At that moment I felt pride knowing I had built this sandbag for myself, just like each workout is foraging my muscles, hardening my spirit.  

Then as easy as it came, the feeling of pride was demolished by an idea *appearing* personal struggle I have encountered throughout my career.

The tools of my trade are computers and code. These have given me a fulfilling career. I am not going to deny this fact. There have been ups and downs that I've rode to the best of my abilities. Even though I feel realized doing what I do, like seasons coming and going, there has been a nagging sensation of *existentialism tied to my work (I am naming it before I describe it, find better wording here*).

Every solution I build is composed of numbers on a screen. Bits and pixels are the only evidence I have from where I stand. Although the code I write does bring positive impact to the patients, I do not see it. I can't hold the solution with myh ink stained hands. 

As I write these words I am holding my journal, my pen. Pages filled with evidence of the work I have done with my writing. Comfort comes back again. Several pages stained with the *strokes* of my life. Experiences turned into ink filled pages.
